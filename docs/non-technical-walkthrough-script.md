# Bridgewater Operations Walkthrough Script

This version matches the current demo story in the system:

- Warren is the main plant that needs attention first.
- Detroit is showing inventory pressure.
- Lansing is strong on production, but delivery is starting to soften.
- Oxford / Eastaboga is the benchmark plant right now.

## Simple Language Guide

- `OEE` = how well the plant is running overall
- `Scrap` = wasted material or lost production
- `OTIF` = whether orders are going out complete and on time
- `Inventory Health` = whether the right parts are in the right place
- `Downtime` = lost time when production is slowed or stopped

## Suggested Demo Path

1. Login page
2. Overview page
3. Warren plant detail page
4. Alerts page
5. Ask AI page

## Walkthrough Script

## 1. Login Page

Click: Sign in with any valid email and any password with at least 6 characters.

Say:

"This opening page is simply the front door to the workspace. For this demo, access is intentionally lightweight, but once we enter, everything is organized around one goal: helping leadership quickly see where attention is needed most across the Bridgewater network."

## 2. Overview Page

Click: Go to the main overview page after login.

Say:

"This is the company-wide view. Instead of looking through separate reports from different plants, leaders can come here and get the full picture in one place."

"Right at the top, the system tells us the main story of the moment. In this case, Warren is driving the most risk in the network, so the system brings that to the surface immediately instead of making someone hunt for it."

"These top scorecards summarize the health of the business in plain terms: how well the plants are running, how much waste is showing up, whether orders are leaving on time, whether parts are balanced, and how much lost production time we are carrying."

"Below that, the plant comparison table lets us see all four facilities side by side. This makes it easy to spot which plant is doing well, which one needs help, and where leadership should focus first."

"The trend charts answer a simple question: are we getting better, getting worse, or staying flat? So this page is not just a snapshot. It also shows direction."

"At the bottom, the alert section and follow-up prompts make it easy to move from a high-level view into the exact issue we want to explore next."

## 3. Warren Plant Detail Page

Click: Open `Warren, MI` from the comparison table or from the top insight card.

Say:

"Now we are moving from the network view into one specific plant. This page is useful because it turns a broad concern into a clear plant story."

"At the top, the system explains the situation in plain language. Warren is dealing with too much downtime, too much scrap, and growing pressure on materials. That means the plant is not just having one bad number. The problems are starting to connect."

"These plant-level scorecards show the same core measures as the main dashboard, but now they are focused on one facility, so managers can quickly see what is slipping."

"The trend charts show whether the plant is recovering or drifting. The downtime mix shows where lost time is coming from, and the inventory section shows whether material coverage and planning confidence are becoming a bigger issue."

"The page also gives suggested follow-up questions, so the user can move naturally from 'What is happening?' to 'Why is it happening?' without leaving the plant context."

## 4. Alerts Page

Click: Open `Alerts` in the left navigation.

Say:

"This page is the watchlist. Instead of reading long reports, the team gets a prioritized list of issues that need attention."

"At the top, we can filter by plant, severity, and type of issue. That means a plant leader, operations leader, or executive can quickly narrow the list to the problems that matter most to them."

"Each alert explains what changed, how serious it is, what likely drivers are behind it, and shows a small chart so we can see the pattern rather than just read a number."

"For example, Warren is showing serious quality and downtime pressure, while Detroit is showing inventory imbalance. So the system is not only saying that something is wrong. It is showing what kind of wrong it is."

"This helps the conversation move from reacting to a red flag into deciding what action to take next."

## 5. Ask AI Page

Click: Open `Ask AI`.

Suggested live question:

`Which Bridgewater facility needs attention first today?`

Say:

"This is the plain-English question page. A manager does not need to know where the data lives or how to build a report. They can just ask a question the way they would ask a person."

"When I ask which facility needs attention first today, the system answers clearly: Warren. It also explains why, using the numbers already in the system."

"What matters here is that the answer is not just a sentence. It also gives the main reasons, shows the plant scope, points to the supporting data, and includes suggested next steps."

"So this is not meant to be a generic chatbot. It is a decision-support tool that helps people move faster from question to understanding."

## Closing

Say:

"In simple terms, this system helps Bridgewater do three things faster. First, it shows where the biggest issue is across the network. Second, it lets users drill into the right plant without wasting time. Third, it lets them ask follow-up questions in everyday language instead of waiting for someone to build a manual report."

"That is the value of the system: less searching, less guessing, and faster action."
